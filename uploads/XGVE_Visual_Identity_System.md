# XGVE Visual Identity System

**Status:** Approved primary identity  
**Canonical master:** `xgve-approved-master-lockup.png`  
**Identity type:** Corporate deep-tech brand with gaming-native energy  
**Primary configuration:** Emblem + compact custom XGVE wordmark on black

---

## 1. Brand idea

XGVE should feel like an experimental technology studio that is credible enough to build serious products, but energetic enough to belong in games, creative technology and frontier software.

The identity combines:

- **Deep-tech precision**
- **Gaming and digital culture**
- **Acceleration and forward motion**
- **Controlled aggression**
- **Corporate restraint**
- **A proprietary monogram rather than a generic mascot**

The logo succeeds because it can be understood immediately as a sharp gaming face or machine visor, while the letters **X, G, V and E** remain embedded inside the construction for viewers who look more closely.

The letters should be discoverable, not explained by additional decoration.

---

## 2. Approved primary lockup

The approved lockup uses:

- A large emblem as the dominant proprietary asset
- A smaller XGVE wordmark tucked tightly into the emblem’s lower-right area
- A primarily white wordmark
- A restrained red accent in the wordmark’s X
- Red concentrated in the emblem’s right eye, needle and acceleration trail
- A pure black background

The wordmark is deliberately subordinate to the emblem. It identifies the company without competing with the symbol.

### Locked compositional decisions

1. **The emblem must remain the visual lead.**
2. **The wordmark remains substantially smaller than the emblem.**
3. **The wordmark sits very close to the emblem.**
4. **Their nominal bounding boxes may overlap, but the visible forms must not touch.**
5. **The wordmark is aligned to the lower visual edge of the emblem rather than vertically centred.**
6. **The red hierarchy remains concentrated in the emblem.**
7. **The primary wordmark remains white apart from the small red X accent.**

Do not enlarge the wordmark to equal the emblem’s visual mass.

## 3. Emblem anatomy

### 3.1 Gaming face and visor

The immediate read is a stylised gaming face, mask or machine visor. This gives the identity character without turning it into a literal mascot.

The two diagonal eye forms connect through a central bridge and should read as one engineered visor component.

The join must be:

- Hard
- Geometric
- Seamless
- Free from glow, blur or feathering

### 3.2 Embedded X

The primary **X** is formed by the connected diagonal visor and the extended lower mouth strokes.

The upper diagonals and lower diagonals create a strong four-direction structure. This gives the emblem stability while preserving speed and aggression.

The X should be readable as a structural consequence of the face, not as a separate letter laid on top.

### 3.3 Embedded G

The broken circular enclosure carries the **G**.

It reads simultaneously as:

- A broken G
- A targeting ring
- A gauge or speedometer
- A system boundary
- A technical interface element

The right-side interruption and inward relationship with the face prevent the circle from becoming a generic badge.

### 3.4 Embedded V

The red right eye and needle create the strongest **V** reading.

The red form functions as:

- An eye
- A check or confirmation mark
- A speedometer needle
- A forward vector
- A launch or acceleration cue

This is the central focus of the emblem and the primary carrier of XGVE’s red signal colour.

### 3.5 Embedded E

The left eye contains three horizontal cuts that create the **E**.

This is the most delicate hidden-letter element and must be protected during vector reconstruction.

Requirements:

- Maintain clear negative-space separation between all three bars
- Keep the middle bar slightly shorter than the upper and lower bars
- Avoid narrowing the gaps until the E becomes a single block
- Preserve the E at small sizes through optical correction rather than literal scaling

The E should remain subtle, but not disappear.

### 3.6 Lower mouth strokes

The lower strokes read as:

- A mouth or jaw
- A second X
- An M-like angular structure
- A stabilising counterweight to the upper visor

Their thickness and chamfer language must match the rest of the emblem. They should feel cut from the same system, not added as a separate symbol.

### 3.7 Acceleration pixels

The red square trail converts the needle into motion.

It suggests:

- Acceleration
- Digital fragmentation
- Rendering or simulation
- Data leaving a system
- Momentum beyond the enclosing ring

The pixels must remain evenly sized and consistently spaced. They should decrease through placement and rhythm, not through random scaling.

Use four squares in the full-size master unless a dedicated small-size optical variant requires reduction.

## 4. Wordmark system

### 4.1 Custom lettering

The XGVE wordmark is custom geometric lettering. It is not a font treatment.

Do not attempt to recreate it by typing XGVE in a commercially available typeface.

Its defining traits are:

- Wide geometric construction
- Angular cuts
- Consistent chamfers
- Open internal counters
- Technical, engineered proportions
- A compact horizontal silhouette
- No curves
- No unnecessary detailing

### 4.2 Letter behaviour

**X**  
The X echoes the emblem’s diagonals. A small red upper-right accent links it to the visor without transferring too much red into the wordmark.

**G**  
The G uses a squared enclosure and inward terminal. It mirrors the emblem’s broken circular G concept in a more typographic form.

**V**  
The V remains white in the approved corporate lockup. This prevents it from competing with the emblem’s red V/needle.

**E**  
The E uses open horizontal construction and angular terminals. It should remain unmistakably legible at compact sizes.

### 4.3 Hierarchy

The wordmark is an identifier, not the hero.

Its job is to:

- Name the brand
- Extend the emblem into a horizontal signature
- Carry the same geometry
- Remain quiet enough for the symbol to dominate

The approved smaller scale and tight placement are intentional.

## 5. Colour system

### Primary palette

| Role | Colour | Hex |
|---|---|---:|
| Void Black | Primary background | `#000000` |
| Signal White | Main structure and wordmark | `#FFFFFF` |
| Signal Red | Visor, needle, trail and X accent | `#E6021B` |

`#E6021B` is a working digital swatch extracted from the approved raster. The final vector master should standardise the red as one flat production swatch.

### Colour hierarchy

Approximate visual priority:

1. Black field
2. White emblem structure
3. Red visor and movement signal
4. White wordmark
5. Small red wordmark accent

Red must remain scarce. Its power comes from concentration.

### Approved colourways

**Primary dark**
- Black background
- White structure
- Signal Red accents

**Light-background adaptation**
- White background
- Black structure and wordmark
- Signal Red accents

**Monochrome white**
- Entire logo white on black or a sufficiently dark field

**Monochrome black**
- Entire logo black on white or a sufficiently light field

The full-colour dark version is the canonical expression.

### Prohibited colour behaviour

- No rainbow gradients
- No blue/purple cyberpunk recolours in the master identity
- No red glow
- No metallic chrome
- No drop shadows
- No bevels
- No translucent visor
- No independently recoloured letters
- No red V in the primary corporate wordmark

## 6. Geometry rules

The final logo must be reconstructed as clean vector geometry rather than image-traced and left uncorrected.

### Required consistency

- Uniform broken-circle thickness
- Consistent diagonal stroke weights
- Matched lower-stroke thicknesses
- Shared chamfer angles
- Mathematically aligned visor endpoints
- Hard white-to-red join
- Even acceleration-pixel sizing and spacing
- Optical centring of the face within the broken enclosure
- Clean intersections without accidental wedges or hairline gaps

### Optical correction

Numerical symmetry is not automatically visual symmetry.

The final vector should be adjusted so that:

- The red side does not visually outweigh the white side
- The lower mouth feels centred beneath the bridge
- The broken circle feels balanced despite its interruptions
- The emblem does not appear to lean right
- The wordmark sits naturally in the emblem’s lower-right visual field

---

## 7. Spacing and alignment

Define **1x** as the thickness of the broken circular enclosure.

### Clear space

- Standard clear space around the complete lockup: **4x**
- Compact interface applications: minimum **2x**
- Emblem-only applications: **3x** preferred

No text, border, icon or image edge may enter this area.

### Emblem-to-wordmark gap

The relationship is intentionally tighter than a conventional corporate lockup.

- Use optical spacing based on the closest visible forms
- Allow the two bounding boxes to interlock
- Never allow the actual white or red shapes to touch
- Do not centre the wordmark vertically against the emblem
- Preserve the lower-edge relationship shown in the approved master

### Alignment

For horizontal placements:

- Align using the emblem’s lower visual stroke and the wordmark baseline region
- Do not align to the broken circle’s numerical centre
- Do not raise the wordmark into the visor

---

## 8. Size system and responsive logo variants

### Full lockup

Recommended minimum digital width: **180 px**

Below this, use the emblem alone unless the application is exceptionally high resolution.

### Standard emblem

Recommended minimum: **32 px**

At 32 px and above:

- Preserve the three-part E
- Retain the acceleration trail where it remains clean
- Maintain the hard centre join

### Small emblem

At **24 px**:

- Use an optically corrected small-size master
- Widen the E gaps
- Slightly simplify tiny interior cuts
- Retain only pixels that render as intentional squares

### Micro mark

At **16 px**:

- Use a dedicated micro variant
- Remove the acceleration pixels if they become noise
- Enlarge the E’s internal gaps
- Simplify the smallest red secondary cut
- Preserve the face, X and broken enclosure first

Never obtain the micro mark by merely shrinking the full vector.

### App icons and avatars

Use the emblem only.

Do not place the full horizontal wordmark inside a square app icon.

Provide versions for:

- 16 px
- 24 px
- 32 px
- 48 px
- 64 px
- 128 px
- 256 px
- 512 px
- 1024 px

## 9. Background and placement

### Preferred environment

The identity is strongest on a pure or near-pure black field with generous negative space.

The black field should feel intentional and premium rather than like an arbitrary dark rectangle.

### Photography and complex backgrounds

Do not place the primary logo directly over noisy photography.

Use one of:

- A black holding field
- A controlled dark gradient field with no colour shift
- A monochrome logo over a quiet high-contrast area

### Edge placement

Keep the acceleration trail away from tight crops. It is part of the silhouette and must not look accidentally cut off.

---

## 10. Supporting typography

The wordmark remains custom artwork.

For broader brand communications, use a restrained supporting system rather than trying to imitate the logo lettering everywhere.

### Recommended hierarchy

- **Display and headings:** Space Grotesk
- **Interface and body:** Inter
- **Technical labels and code:** IBM Plex Mono

Alternative equivalent families may be used when licensing or platform constraints require them, provided they remain:

- Clean
- Geometric
- Highly legible
- Modern
- Low in personality compared with the emblem

Avoid using highly stylised sci-fi fonts for body copy.

The logo provides the character; supporting typography provides clarity.

---

## 11. Graphic language

The surrounding design system should borrow from the logo without copying it literally.

### Approved motifs

- Sharp diagonal cuts
- Broken circular progress rings
- Sparse red status indicators
- Square pixel trails
- Thin technical rules
- Large black fields
- White geometric panels
- Controlled red confirmation states
- Cropped or partial ring geometry
- Angular transitions between sections

### Restraint rule

Use one or two motifs at a time.

Do not fill every surface with:

- Visors
- Broken circles
- Pixel trails
- Red diagonals
- Hidden letters

The identity is strong because the mark concentrates these ideas in one place.

---

## 12. Motion principles

Motion should reinforce acceleration and precision.

### Appropriate motion

- Red pixels advancing outward from the needle
- A progress sweep moving through the broken ring
- A fast hard reveal along the visor angle
- A brief digital assembly into the final emblem
- Controlled cursor-like or system-status pulses

### Inappropriate motion

- Bouncing
- Cartoon squash and stretch
- Continuous spinning
- Flame effects
- Excessive glitching
- Rainbow chromatic aberration
- Long particle explosions

Animation should end on a perfectly still, sharp logo.

---

## 13. Tone of voice

The visual identity implies a verbal tone that is:

- Experimental
- Direct
- Technically literate
- Confident
- Slightly provocative
- Concise
- Forward-looking

Avoid:

- Forced hacker slang
- Generic “future of everything” claims
- Esports shouting
- Excessive military language
- Empty corporate innovation language
- Explaining the hidden letters in everyday marketing

The monogram’s layered meaning is a discovery reward, not required reading.

---

## 14. Correct usage

Use the complete lockup when:

- Introducing the company
- Leading a website or presentation
- Signing formal communications
- Appearing in sponsorship or partner contexts
- Establishing first recognition

Use the emblem alone when:

- Space is square or compact
- The brand is already established in context
- Used as an app icon, favicon or avatar
- Used as a watermark
- Used as a navigation or loading symbol

---

## 15. Incorrect usage

Never:

- Separate or rearrange the emblem’s internal components
- Add more hidden symbols
- Turn the eye into a literal illustrated eye
- Close the broken G enclosure
- Remove the lower X/mouth strokes
- Change the E to three equal solid bars with no negative space
- Soften the central visor join
- Enlarge the wordmark until it competes with the emblem
- Increase the emblem-to-wordmark gap
- Vertically centre the wordmark against the circle
- Make the primary V red
- Randomise the acceleration pixels
- Add outlines, shadows, gradients or glow
- Stretch, skew or rotate the complete lockup
- Re-type the wordmark in a substitute font
- Place a tagline inside the logo’s clear space

Most importantly: **do not add another visual idea.**

The emblem already carries a face, X, G, V, E, visor, gauge and acceleration cue. Additional symbolism would reduce rather than increase distinctiveness.

Before approving the vector master, confirm:

- [ ] Emblem remains dominant

- [ ] Lower alignment matches the approved master
- [ ] White-to-red visor join is perfectly hard
- [ ] Left-eye E remains legible
- [ ] E middle bar is optically shorter
- [ ] Circle thickness is consistent
- [ ] Chamfer angles are shared
- [ ] Lower strokes have matched weight
- [ ] Acceleration pixels are even
- [ ] Red is flat and standardised
- [ ] Wordmark V remains white
- [ ] Wordmark has only the restrained red X accent

- [ ] 16, 24, 32, 48 and 64 px tests have been performed
- [ ] Monochrome and light-background versions remain recognisable
- [ ] All vector paths are clean and editable
- [ ] No extra symbolism has been introduced

## 18. One-sentence identity definition

**XGVE is an engineered deep-tech monogram disguised as a fast, game-native machine face: a broken G enclosure, structural X, red V needle and embedded E moving beyond the system that contains it.**
